If you, or someone else, repeats a word and phrase enough times in a brief period, you'll likely experience [semantic satiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semantic_satiation). Perception becomes warped, and all of a sudden where there was once meaning, there is now just sound.

I think this happens at a conceptual level too, over a long period. I believe that many people learn the definition of a word, but the context in which they learned that word's meaning becomes an anchor.

Overall harmless.

But there are many words and phrases that, through immense repetition and contextual shaping, become [loaded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loaded_language). Loaded language ranges broadly from swear words to the [catchphrases of politicians.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loaded_language#Examples) This type of "semantic satiation", I believe, is only beneficial to people who use it to control and manipulate.

With enough scale and time, some words and phrases gain enough baggage that they become difficult to even talk about. The mere mention of *religion* sends many people I've met into a combative stance, ready for some debate where they will defend or attack a conceptual position. The other chunk of people claim [agnosticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agnosticism), so they might politely disengage and avoid discussing it altogether. 

I think this type of satiation is tragic.

This article is about some of my favorite words, their etymology, and how historical meanings have really impacted how I make sense of the world.

# What is Etymology?

I believe that through just light engagement with [etymology](https://www.etymonline.com/search?q=etymology) (etymon "true sense; original meaning" + logia "study of"), the world can really start to open up to you in a myriad of different ways.

To me, etymology has been a great way to break out of my personal bubble of understanding. Words gain new meaning over time, this is inevitable, but that drift papers over historical usage. Etymology almost feels like a way to understand people of the past.

> [!tip]- A quote from that *etymonline* weblink that says it better than I can
> "As practised by Socrates in the _Cratylus_, etymology involves a claim about the underlying semantic content of the name, what it really means or indicates. This content is taken to have been put there by the ancient namegivers: giving an etymology is thus a matter of unwrapping or decoding a name to find the message the namegivers have placed inside. [Rachel Barney, "Socrates Agonistes: The Case of the _Cratylus_ Etymologies," in "Oxford Studies in Ancient Philosophy," vol. xvi, 1998]"
